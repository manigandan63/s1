Cholesterol

What is cholesterol?

Cholesterol is a waxy, fat-like substance that your body needs to stay healthy, but when there is too much of it in the blood, it builds up on the walls of the arteries.1 Over time, this build-up, called plaque, causes hardening and narrowing of the arteries so that blood flow is slowed down or blocked.2 If the blood supply to a portion of the heart is completely blocked, the result is a heart attack.2

High cholesterol is one of the major risk factors for developing heart disease, heart attack and stroke.2,3

Good versus bad cholesterol

The liver and other cells in the body make about 75% of the body’s cholesterol.4 The other 25% comes from animal products in the diet.4 Many people inherit genes from their parents that increase cholesterol levels (i.e. familial high cholesterol).5

There are two types of cholesterol:2

‘Bad’ cholesterol or LDL cholesterol – which can clog arteries and increase the risk of heart attack and stroke.2

‘Good’ cholesterol or HDL cholesterol – which helps prevent the ‘bad’ cholesterol from getting lodged in the walls of the arteries2 Triglycerides are another form of fat in the blood that, when elevated are also associated with heart attacks and strokes.2,4

A blood test that your doctor can perform can provide information about your:2

Total cholesterol

LDL (bad) cholesterol

HDL (good) cholesterol

Triglycerides

Too little ‘good’ cholesterol as well as too much ‘bad’ cholesterol increases the risk for heart attack and stroke.3 Cholesterol – know your numbers. High cholesterol itself does not cause any symptoms, so many people are unaware that their cholesterol levels are too high.2 In South Africa, 1 in 100 people have familial high cholesterol. Therefore, South African cholesterol guidelines recommend that everyone should have their cholesterol level tested at least once in young adulthood (from 20 years of age).5 The cholesterol screening test is usually done after a 9 to 12 hour fast.2 The test report shows your cholesterol levels in millimoles per litre (mmol/L) of blood.6

There are two types of cholesterol:2  
Total cholesterol < 5 mmol/L < 5 mmol/L
LDL cholesterol* < 3 mmol/L < 3 mmol/L
HDL cholesterol (women) > 1.2 mmol/L > 1.2 mmol/L
HDL cholesterol (men) > 1.0 mmol/L > 1.0 mmol/L
Triglycerides < 1.7 mmol/L < 1.7 mmol/L

*In patients at high risk of heart disease, the LDL cholesterol goal should be lower (< 2.5 mmol/L). In patients at very high risk of heart disease, the LDL cholesterol goal should be even lower (< 1.8 mmol/L).5,6

To find out how your cholesterol levels affect your risk of heart disease, your doctor will also consider other risk factors like your age, family history, smoking and blood pressure.5

Are you at high risk for a heart attack or stroke?

Very high-risk individuals include those with:5

A history of heart attack, stroke or circulatory disease

Diabetes mellitus

Familial high cholesterol

Kidney disease

In addition to high cholesterol, there are several other factors that increase the risk of heart disease:5,7

High blood pressure

Smoking

Overweight/obesity

Family history of heart disease (male before 55 years of age; female before 60 years of age)

Advancing age

Male gender

How can cholesterol be lowered?

Cholesterol levels can almost always be lowered with a combination of diet, weight loss, exercise and medications.3 As cholesterol levels fall, so does the risk of developing heart disease.3 This may result in a lower risk of suffering a heart attack or stroke.3 It is not too late if heart disease is already present because cholesterol-lowering treatment can be lifesaving.3

Lowering high cholesterol is important for people at all ages, with and without established heart disease.1

Limit saturated fats and trans fats5,8

Foods high in saturated fat include fatty meat, chicken skin, processed meats, bacon, sausages, whole milk, cream and butter.

Remove all visible fat before cooking and avoid deep-frying food.

Trans fats may be found in hard margarines, shortening, fried foods and packaged foods made with hydrogenated oils.

Eat fewer baked goods e.g. biscuits, cakes, pastries.

Eat healthy fats (polyunsaturated and monounsaturated)2,9

Choose fat-free or low-fat dairy products, lean meats, skinless chicken and fish.

Use soft tub margarines e.g. those approved by the Heart Foundation of South Africa.

Cook with oils such as olive, sunflower, canola and peanut oil.

Limit the amount of cholesterol you eat.8

Foods high in cholesterol include egg yolks, fatty meat, whole milk, cheese, liver, shrimp, lobster and crab.

Eat more omega-3 fats (heart-healthy fats)8

Aim to eat fish twice a week.

Good choices include salmon, tuna, mackerel and sardines.

Flaxseed is another source of omega-3 fats Dietary tips to reduce cholesterol.5,8,9

Eat more plant-based foods

Use beans and soy foods for protein.8

Good choices include salmon, tuna, mackerel and sardines.

Eat at least 5 servings of fruits and/or vegetables daily.9

Replace all refined carbohydrates with food high in fibre.5

Avoid eating products made from white flour, such as white bread, pizzas, vetkoek, samosas.

Eat more wholegrain foods such as oats, barley, crushed wheat, samp, brown rice, whole-grain breakfast cereals, health and seed breads.

Avoid foods high in free sugars, such as:5

Sweets, chocolates, fizzy soft drinks, fruit juices, flavoured and sweetened waters, low-fat sweetened milky drinks.

If you consume alcohol.5

Do so in moderation; no more than 2 drinks for men and 1 drink for women per day

Avoid adding salt to food after cooking.5

Choose and prepare foods with little or no salt by using more herbs and spices.

For dietary lifestyle intervention, all patients should ideally consult a registered dietician.5

Lifestyle changes to reduce cholesterol?

Besides eating a heart-healthy diet, everyone with high cholesterol should:1,2,3,5

Achieve and maintain a healthy weight.

Exercise regularly (at least 30 minutes on all or most days of the week).

Stop smoking

The benefits of these lifestyle changes are usually noticed within 6 to 12 months, but the success rates vary widely, and doctors sometimes recommend starting cholesterol-lowering medicines before this time is over.3

If you have high cholesterol, you will need to eat a healthy diet, exercise regularly, stop smoking and you may need to take medication.1

High cholesterol may have no symptoms, but the disease is lifelong.10

It is important to continue with lifestyle changes and taking your prescribed medicines to lower cholesterol.2

Medicines to lower cholesterol

There are many medicines available to help lower cholesterol levels.3 Your doctor will recommend a medicine, or a combination of medicines based on your cholesterol level and other individual factors.3

Medicine What it does
Statin Decreases the body’s production of cholesterol.3 Can reduce LDL-cholesterol and triglycerides.3 Can slightly increase HDL-cholesterol levels.3
Cholesterol absorption inhibitor Reduces the body’s ability to absorb cholesterol from food as well as cholesterol that the body makes.3 Can reduce LDL cholesterol levels.3
Bile acid sequestrants Binds to bile acids in the intestine, reducing the amount of cholesterol absorbed from foods.3
Fibrates Can lower triglyceride levels and raise HDL cholesterol levels.3

High cholesterol may have no symptoms, but the disease is lifelong.10 It is important to continue with lifestyle changes and taking your prescribed medicines to lower cholesterol.2
