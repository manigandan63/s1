Vaccines

At MSD

MSD has a long and rich history of working to improve people's health and well-being. Through the years, our researchers have helped to find new ways to treat and prevent illness – from the discovery of vitamin B1, to the first measles vaccine, to cold remedies and antacids, to the first statins to treat high cholesterol. Our scientists also have helped develop many products to improve animal health, including vaccines and antibiotics. Vaccines and clean water have done more to improve human health than any other intervention.

We have 3 categories of vaccines:

Infant vaccines designed to prevent common childhood diseases. These include the MMR to prevent measles, mumps and rubella, the varicella vaccine to prevent chickenpox and the rotavirus vaccine to prevent the severe diarrhoea due to the virus. They are all very effective and are not associated with autism. Prevention of these childhood diseases saves lives and children do not grow up with vaccine preventable consequences.

Adolescent vaccines that include the vaccine to prevent human papilloma virus (HPV) which is sexually transmitted. This vaccine is best if given before sexual debut but is effective regardless of the persons age or sex. By preventing infection with certain high-risk strains, known to be associated with cancer, it is reducing the future development of cervical cancer which is still a serious problem in many countries including sub-Sahara Africa. It is also effective at reducing genital warts by 90% in sexually active persons, which is an underreported and seldom discussed problem.

Adult vaccines include those to prevent pneumonia caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae in at-risk patients and a vaccine to prevent shingles which is a flare up of the chickenpox virus. Anybody at risk is eligible to receive the vaccines, but it is usually recommended for older adults.
